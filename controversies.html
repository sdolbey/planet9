The case for and against Pluto

For

  • Pluto has many similarities to other planets: Pluto has a spherical shape, a moon. And an atmosphere
  • No two planets are exactly alike: Each of the other 8 planets have an extreme range of diversity between them. For instance, the planets and Mercury and Jupiter are much more different than Pluto is to the other rocky planets
  • Planets should not have to dominate their locality: Historically, planetary bodies are judged based on their characteristics not their location
  • Pluto has a unique and complex landscape: The New Horizons mission found nitrogen rich ice-plains as well as vast mountain regions on Pluto
  • Pluto has an atmosphere: Pluto has an atmosphere very similar to the atmosphere of Earth

Against

  • Pluto is similar to objects in the Keiper Belt: When the question was asked, can the existence of one object demand its own category, astronomers detected that Pluto was similar to other objects in the Keiper Belt
  • Charon’s location causes problems: While other planets are much larger than their moons, Charon is almost half the size of Pluto
  • Pluto is not unique: There are many planetary bodies in our solar system similar to Pluto and with the new definition, these objects can also be classified
  • Eris to outrank Pluto: In 2005 a astronomical body, Eris, was found to be larger than Pluto

In the year of 2006, when Pluto was demoted to Dwarf Planet, the main reasoning behind it was due to the planet not having a clear orbit aka be the largest gravitational force in its orbit. A few years fast forward, Dr. Philip Metzger who has made a massive impact in the space community as well as being a UCF alumni and planetary scientist at the University, reopened the discussion that the reasoning behind pluto's demotion was uncalled for and called for a changing in the definition of a planet. Metzger says that “the definition of a planet should be based on its intrinsic properties, rather than ones that can change, such as the dynamics of a planet’s orbit.” Metzger recommends classifying a planet on whether or not it's a spherical shape in space. He also discusses the habitat of Pluto stating how it has a much wider diversity in its ecosystem compared to Mars.

For more information regarding Metzgers Articles and research, check out his personal article “Nine reasons why pluto is a planet”.

Issue: “Clearing your neighborhood” requirement

For

  • Earth, Mars, Jupiter, and Neptune all share their orbits with asteroids
  • No planet truly clears its orbit

Against

  • Pluto’s mass is significantly less than the combined mass of other objects in its orbit

According to Alan Stern, principal investigator with NASA’s New Horizons mission to Pluto, there are technical reasons why the 2006 IAU definition of a planet isn’t adequate. He says the third condition, which states that a planet must “have cleared the neighborhood around its orbit”, is vague, and that Earth, Mars, Jupiter, and Neptune have not cleared their neighborhoods either. Earth, for example, co-orbits with 10,000 near-Earth asteroids. He argues that, “If Neptune had cleared its zone, Pluto wouldn’t be there.”

Philip Metzger, planetary scientist and University of Central Florida lecturer, also takes issue with the clearing your neighborhood requirement. He argues that no planet actually clears its orbit, so therefore there are no planets.
